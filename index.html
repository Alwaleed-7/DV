Chapter 1

Introduction to Data Visualization

Definition and Importance

Data visualization is the graphical representation of information and data using charts and images. It creates visual representations that communicate complex information in an easy-to-understand manner and helps to uncover hidden patterns, correlations, and outliers.

Key Points:

  • "Data is the new oil" – a cliché but true; raw data is worthless until refined, analyzed, and understood (Disney 2017)
  • Data is only as good as your ability to understand and communicate it
  • By presenting data visually, it becomes easier to identify patterns, trends, outliers, and relationships
  • Data visualization allows non-experts to analyze data at scale and extract potentially complex insights
  • Critical: Choose the right method for data visualization. For example, trends cannot be shown using a pie chart
  • It reveals insights and patterns that are not immediately visible in the raw data
  • Speed: Humans process visuals 60,000 times faster than text

Why Data Visualization is Important

  • Sense-making: Crucial for making sense of large and complex data sets
  • Pattern identification: Quickly identify patterns and trends difficult to see in tables/spreadsheets
  • Accessibility: Simplifies complex information for wider audiences, especially non-experts
  • Speed: Human brains excel at identifying patterns in tangible formats
  • Trend detection: Easy to get trends and outliers using charts
  • Impact: Improves the impact and recall of your message or argument

Types of Data

1. Based on Nature

A) Qualitative (Categorical) Data:
  • Nominal: Categories without order (e.g., gender, religion, eye color)
  • Ordinal: Categories with order/ranking (e.g., education level, satisfaction scale)
B) Quantitative (Numerical) Data:
  • Discrete: Countable numbers (e.g., number of students, goals scored)
  • Continuous: Measurable on a scale, can take any value (e.g., height, weight, temperature)

2. Based on Source

  • Primary Data: Collected first-hand (e.g., surveys, experiments)
  • Secondary Data: Already collected by others (e.g., books, reports, online databases)

3. Based on Measurement Scale (Stevens' Classification)

  • Nominal: Labels or names (no order)
  • Ordinal: Ordered categories
  • Interval: Numeric, equal intervals but no true zero (e.g., temperature in °C)
  • Ratio: Numeric, equal intervals with true zero (e.g., age, income, weight)

4. Based on Structure

  • Structured Data: Organized in rows & columns (e.g., databases, spreadsheets)
  • Unstructured Data: No fixed format (e.g., text, images, videos)
  • Semi-Structured Data: Some organization but not strict (e.g., JSON, XML, logs)

Types of Data Visualization

1. Based on Data Dimension

A) Univariate Visualization – Shows one variable at a time
  • Histogram: Frequency distribution of a single variable
  • Box Plot: Distribution, median, quartiles, outliers
  • Bar Chart: Comparison of categories
B) Bivariate Visualization – Shows relationship between two variables
  • Scatter Plot: Relationship/correlation between two numeric variables
  • Line Chart: Trend over time
  • Grouped Bar Chart: Compare two categorical variables
C) Multivariate Visualization – Shows relationships among three or more variables
  • Bubble Chart: Adds size to scatter plot to represent a third variable
  • Heatmap: Color intensity shows values across two dimensions
  • Stacked Bar Chart: Shows contribution of multiple variables

2. Based on Purpose

  • Comparison: Compare data points or categories (Bar charts, Column charts, Bullet charts)
  • Distribution: Show data distribution and spread (Histograms, Box plots, Violin plots)
  • Relationship/Correlation: Show relationship between variables (Scatter plots, Bubble charts, Line charts)
  • Composition: Show parts of a whole (Pie charts, Stacked area charts, Treemaps)

3. Specialized Visualizations

  • Time Series: Trends over time (Line charts, Area charts)
  • Geospatial: Data on maps (Choropleth maps, Heat maps, Dot maps)
  • Network/Flow: Connections or processes (Sankey diagrams, Network graphs, Flow charts)

Best Practices for Data Visualization

When creating data visualizations, follow these best practices to ensure effective communication:

  • Simplicity: Keep design simple and uncluttered
  • Appropriate elements: Use appropriate colors and fonts
  • Clear labeling: Label axes and legends clearly
  • Aesthetic considerations: Color visibility and text specifications are very important
  • Audience awareness: Consider the audience - provide more context for non-technical audiences
  • Consistency: Maintain consistent design elements

Tools for Data Visualization

Examples: Tableau, Power BI, Google Charts, Python (Matplotlib, Seaborn), R (ggplot2)

Factors to consider: Ease of use, cost, features, suitability for data types (e.g., GIS for geospatial)

Applications

  • Exploratory data analysis
  • Mapping geographic data
  • Showing change over time
  • Comparing values across categories
  • Showing connections and flows

Conclusion

Data visualization enhances understanding but is not a substitute for critical analysis.

Chapter 2

Data Visualization Principles and Categories

Data Visualization Principles

Data visualization is not just about making pretty graphs; it's about making data accessible, understandable, and actionable. There are principles serving as guidance to authors who seek to improve their visual representation:

1. Diagram First

Least technical but very important factor. First envision the information you want to share mentally or on paper. Think about the core information being conveyed and your visual objective: comparison, ranking, or composition.

2. Choose the Right Software

Command over 1 or 2 visualization tools/software is essential. Have in-depth knowledge of at least one tool known for drawing complex figures. Choose the most suitable tool for your needs.

3. Use of Effective Geometry

Effectively use shapes and features in your figures. Consider geometries based on objectives:

  • Amounts/Comparisons: Generally represented using bars and line charts
  • Compositions/Proportions: Wide range but generally use pie charts
  • Distributions: Data scattering shown using box plots and density plots
  • Relationships: Correlation between data attributes using scatter plots, heat maps

4. Use of Appropriate Colors and Aesthetics

Proper use of color can make figures incredibly powerful because each color represents a category of information.

  • Colorful visualizations have higher memorability scores
  • Avoid unnecessary use of too many colors
  • Three major color schemes: Sequential, Diverging, Qualitative
  • Design color figures that can be converted to effective grayscale
  • Use color schemes effective for colorblind readers (ColorBrewer tool)
  • Color transparency is very important

5. Context and Consistency

Provide appropriate context by providing appropriate labels, legends, and other elements. Maintain consistency in design elements throughout visualization. A plotted model should be provided with explanation for readers to fully reproduce the work.

6. Simple Values and Detailed Caption

Figure captions should be standalone. Have detailed captions that fully explain everything in the figure. A study found that one-third of graphs are not self-explanatory.

7. Get Feedback

Most effective visuals are easy to understand and easily connect with readers. Get review from one or more colleagues or people external to the study.

How to Choose the Right Visualization Technique

Data Type

The nature of your data—whether categorical, numerical, or temporal—significantly influences your choice. Bar charts are excellent for categorical data, while line charts are ideal for temporal data trends.

Purpose and Audience

Understand the visualization's objective and the audience's familiarity with data. Technical audiences might include more detailed representations, while general audiences benefit from straightforward, intuitive visuals.

Data Volume and Granularity

Amount of data and detail level affect choice. For large datasets, summary visualizations like histograms or box plots might be more effective. For small datasets, scatter plots or line graphs work well.

Comparison vs. Distribution

Different visualizations serve different purposes. Bar and pie charts work for comparing categories. Histograms and box plots are appropriate for understanding data distribution.

Trends and Patterns

Line or area charts are suitable for highlighting trends over time. Scatter plots or heat maps can be effective for identifying patterns or correlations.

Clarity and Simplicity

Visualization should be as simple as possible while conveying necessary information. Avoid overloading viewers with too much information or overly complex visuals.

Interactivity

Interactive visualizations on digital platforms can enhance user engagement and allow deeper data exploration. Dashboards often incorporate interactive elements.

Tools and Resources

Choice can depend on available tools and user's technical proficiency. Advanced visualizations might require specialized software and skills.

Categories of Data Visualization

Chart

Charts are used for comparing discrete values or proportions across categories. Present information in tabular, graphical form with data displayed along two axes. Can be graphs, diagrams, or maps.

Examples: Bar charts, pie charts, column charts

Examples: Line graphs, scatter plots, area graphs

Geospatial Figures/Maps

Show data associated with locations.

Examples: Heatmaps, dot maps, proportional symbol maps

Dashboard

A collection of plots, charts, and data displays that allow for interactive analysis.

Categorization by Purpose/Task:

  • Comparison: Bar/grouped bar, pie
  • Relationship: Scatter, bubble, heatmaps
  • Trend: Line, area, timelines
  • Distribution: Histograms, box/whisker

Chapter 3

Data Preprocessing and Transformation Techniques

Data Preprocessing Overview

Data preprocessing is a process applied to raw data to make it ready for further analysis or processing tasks. It converts raw data into a format that can be processed more efficiently and accurately in tasks such as data analysis, machine learning, data science, and AI.

Major Tasks:

  • Data cleaning
  • Data integration
  • Data transformation
  • Data reduction

Importance:

Ensures data quality in terms of accuracy, completeness, consistency, timeliness, believability, and interpretability.

Tools:

OpenRefine, Trifacta, Tibco Clarity, Ringlead, Talend, Paxata, Cloudingo, Jupyter Notebooks

Data Cleaning

Data cleaning is the process of adding missing data and correcting, repairing, or removing incorrect or irrelevant data from a dataset. It is the most important step of preprocessing because it ensures your data is ready to go.

It involves identifying and correcting errors or inconsistencies to ensure data is accurate and complete. This includes handling missing values, removing duplicates, and correcting incorrect or outlier data.

Missing Values

Process of identifying values that are absent in the dataset.

Reasons for Missing Values:
  • Past data might get corrupted
  • Data not recorded
  • Intentional non-response
Representation:

As NaN, NULL, special characters

Handling Methods:
  • Delete: Remove rows/columns with missing values
  • Imputation: Fill with mean/median/mode, KNN, statistical prediction

Note: Missing values can affect analytical results, so handle carefully.

Noisy Data

Irrelevant or incorrect data that is difficult for machines to process. Includes random errors (typos, sensor malfunctions), outliers, irrelevant features, inconsistencies, and natural variability.

Causes:
  • Data collection errors (faulty instruments, human error)
  • Transmission issues
  • Processing errors
Handling Methods:
Binning

Grouping data points into "bins" and smoothing values by replacing them with bin mean/median/boundaries.

Approaches: Smoothing by bin means, median, boundaries

Regression

Fitting data to a function (linear/multiple regression)

Clustering

Group similar data points, detect outliers

Removing Duplicates

Identifying and eliminating repeated data entries to ensure accuracy.

Data Integration

Data integration is a method of processing data from multiple heterogeneous sources and combining them coherently to retain a unified view of information. Sources may include multiple data cubes, databases, or flat files.

Triple (G, S, M) Approach:

  • G: Global schema
  • S: Heterogeneous source of schema
  • M: Mapping between source and global schema queries

Challenges:

Data integration can be challenging due to variety of data formats, structures, and semantics. Different sources may use different data types, naming conventions, and schemas, making it difficult to combine data into a single view.

Process:

Typically involves manual and automated processes including data profiling, data mapping, data transformation, and data reconciliation.

Data Transformation

Data transformation is the process of converting raw data into a cleansed and validated form. It involves reorganizing or restructuring data to make it more efficient for analysis. It is also part of ETL (Extract, Transform, Load).

Types/Techniques:

  • Format conversion
  • Cleaning
  • Normalization/standardization
  • Aggregation
  • Merging/splitting
  • Encoding/decoding
  • Validation

Smoothing Methods:

Binning, Clustering, Regression, Moving Average

Other Transformation Techniques:

Attribute Construction

Creating new attributes from existing ones (e.g., area = height × width)

Generalization

Moving from low-level to high-level using hierarchies (e.g., age → youth/old)

Aggregation

Summarizing data using sum, average, count, min/max, grouping

Discretization

Converting continuous to discrete using equal-width/frequency, histogram analysis, clustering, decision trees, entropy-based methods

Normalization

Scaling data using:

  • Decimal scaling: Moving decimal point
  • Min-max: Scaling to [0,1] range
  • Z-score: Using mean and standard deviation

Data Reduction

Reducing data size while preserving information. Reduces volume and dimensions.

Purpose:

  • Reduce storage and computation cost
  • Simplify analysis
  • Remove redundant features
  • Improve algorithm performance

Techniques:

Dimensionality Reduction

Remove irrelevant/redundant features using PCA, Feature Selection, Factor Analysis

Numerosity Reduction

Create smaller representation using Histograms, Clustering, Sampling, Aggregation

Data Compression

Encoding techniques:

  • Lossless: ZIP, run-length encoding
  • Lossy: Discard some data but preserve patterns
Discretization and Concept Hierarchy

As described in transformation section above

Visual Encoding: Marks and Channels

Definitions: Marks

A mark is a basic graphical element in an image, serving as the building blocks of any chart or graph. Marks are geometric primitives classified by the number of dimensions they require: points (0D), lines (1D), areas (2D), and volumes (3D). Volumes are rarely used in 2D visualizations due to their complexity and limited applicability in flat media.

Key Points:

  • Points (0D): Represent individual data points (e.g., dots in scatter plots).
  • Lines (1D): Connect data points to show trends (e.g., line charts).
  • Areas (2D): Fill space to emphasize magnitude (e.g., area charts).
  • Mark = What you draw: The visual symbols representing data values.

In data visualization, marks represent data values visually, acting as the fundamental shapes or symbols that convey information on the screen or page.

Common Types of Marks:

Mark Type Example Visualization Description
Point Scatter plot Represents individual data points (e.g., dots, circles).
Line Line chart Connects data points to show trends or changes over time.
Area Area chart Fills space under a line to emphasize magnitude.
Bar (or Rect) Bar chart Represents quantity or frequency with length or height.
Text Label charts Displays data as words or numbers directly.
Tick Dot plot, box plot Represents position or value along a scale.

Mark Types in Datasets: In table datasets, marks represent items (e.g., rows). In network datasets, marks can represent items (nodes) or links. There are two types of link marks: connection (pairwise relationships using lines) and containment (hierarchical relationships using areas, which can be nested).

Constraints on Marks: Different marks have size constraints. For example, area marks have both dimensions fixed by shape (e.g., map regions); line marks can vary width but not length if encoding quantity; point marks can vary size and shape freely.

Definitions: Channels

A visual channel is a way to control the appearance of marks, independent of the dimensionality of their geometric primitives. Channels encode differences in data values.

Key Points:

  • Common Channels: Position (x, y), color (hue), shape, tilt (angle/orientation), size (length, area, volume).
  • Other Channels: Depth, luminance, curvature, etc.
  • Channel = How you show data differences: Together with marks, they form the visual language of data visualization.

Example: In a scatter plot, points (marks) can encode: x-position = age, y-position = income, color = gender.

Adding Channels to Visualizations: Bar charts start with categorical (x) and quantitative (y height). Add horizontal position for second quantitative, hue for categorical, size for another quantitative.

Single vs. Multiple Channel Encoding:

  • Single Channel: Uses one visual property (e.g., bar height for quantity in a bar chart).
  • Multiple Channel: Uses two or more properties (e.g., scatter plot with position, color, size encoding multiple attributes).

Human Perceptual System: Divided into identity channels (what/where: shapes, colors, motion) and magnitude channels (how much: length, size, brightness). Identity for categorical, magnitude for quantitative/ordinal.

Using Marks and Channels

The design should be guided by principles of expressiveness and effectiveness, creating channel rankings based on data type. Assign highest-ranked channels to most important attributes.

Expressiveness and Effectiveness

Expressiveness: Accurately represents data meaning—shows all necessary information without misleading implications.

Example: Use hue for categories (expressive); not for quantities (not expressive, as hue doesn't imply order).

Expressive Encodings: Position/length for quantitative; hue/shape for categorical.

Effectiveness: How easily/accurately viewers interpret data. Position > color for comparisons.

Effective Encodings (Quantitative): Position (most accurate), length, angle, area, color saturation (least).

Expressiveness Principle: Encode all attribute info; show ordered data as ordered, unordered without implying order. Match identity channels to categorical, magnitude to ordinal/quantitative.

Data Type Meaning Example Suitable Visual Encodings
Ordered Values have natural sequence or ranking Age, temperature, income Position, length, size, color saturation
Unordered Categories have no inherent order Gender, fruit type, department Color hue, shape, separate groupings

Example: Ordered - bar chart with monthly sales ordered chronologically; Unordered - pie chart with product categories using colors.

Effectiveness Principle: Match attribute importance to channel salience; most important gets most effective channel.

Channel Ranking

Hierarchy of channels for data types. Spatial channels (position) top for both quantitative and categorical. Others vary; 3D depth lower ranked.

Channel Effectiveness

Analyzed by accuracy, discriminability, separability, visual popout, perceptual grouping.

Accuracy

How close perceptual judgment is to objective measure. Governed by Stevens’ Power Law: S = I^n (n>1 magnified, n<1 compressed, n=1 unchanged).

Human perception follows power laws; most stimuli magnified/compressed.

Discriminability

Perceiving intended differences in encoded attributes. Quantifies distinguishable bins/levels per channel.

Factor Description Example
Color contrast Colors must be sufficiently different Red vs. green is more discriminable than light red vs. pink
Size difference Bigger differences = easier to perceive Circle sizes: 10px vs. 12px are hard to distinguish
Shape distinctiveness Clear geometric differences Circle vs. square = good; circle vs. oval = poor
Spatial separation Distance between items Overlapping points are harder to separate
Visual clutter Too many elements reduce discriminability Dense scatterplot = poor discriminability

Examples: Sized points/lines limited to ~3 distinguishable sizes; aggregated ranges.

Separability

Channels have interactions: separable (independent, e.g., position + hue) vs. integral (fused, e.g., horizontal + vertical size perceived as area).

Examples: Position + hue: easy to separate categories; size + hue: harder within sizes; horizontal/vertical size: fuses to area (3 groups); red/green: inseparable (hard to decode).

Matching: Use separable for independent attributes; integral for combined perception.

Visual Popout

Making items stand out instantly (preattentive processing). Independent of distractor count.

Examples: Red circle pops from blue circles (small/large sets); slower from squares; pops from mixed but not from red squares + blue circles (requires search).

Depends on channel and difference; single channel at a time. Supports: tilt, size, shape, proximity, shadow direction. Not: parallel lines.

Visual Grouping

From link marks (containment strongest, then connection) or identity channels (selective attention).

Principle How It Works Example
Proximity Elements close together are seen as belonging together. Dots placed near each other form clusters.
Similarity Elements that look alike (color, shape, size) are grouped. Blue circles and red squares form two groups.
Continuity The eye follows smooth lines or paths, perceiving them as a group. Points forming a curve appear connected.
Closure The brain fills in missing parts to perceive a whole shape. A broken circle is seen as complete.
Common Region Elements within the same bounded area are grouped. Items inside a box appear related.
Connectedness Physically linked items are perceived as one group. Two dots connected by a line appear paired.

Relative vs. Absolute Judgments

Absolute: Interpret property without comparison (e.g., estimate bar length directly).

Relative: Compare elements (e.g., one bar taller than another).

Human perception based on relative judgments. Weber’s Law: δI/I = K (difference proportional to magnitude).

Example: Position on scale more accurate than isolated length. Color masking shows context effects (yellow light: orange; blue light: purple).

Conclusion

Marks and channels, when used with expressiveness and effectiveness principles, create powerful, accurate visualizations. Match channels to data types, consider perceptual criteria, and prioritize important attributes for optimal design.

Definition and Importance

Multivariate Data Visualization is the graphical representation and analysis of datasets involving more than two variables simultaneously. It extends beyond univariate (one variable) and bivariate (two variables) analysis to uncover complex patterns, correlations, and relationships in multidimensional data.

Key Points:

  • Categories of Analysis: Univariate (one variable), Bivariate (two variables), Multivariate (more than two variables).
  • Simultaneous Examination: Analyzes patterns and correlations among multiple variables at once for deeper understanding.
  • Real-World Complexity: Concepts like self-esteem are influenced by multiple factors (e.g., age, employment, exercise, relationship status), making multivariate analysis essential.
  • Correlation: Measures relationships from -1 (strong negative) to +1 (strong positive).
  • Benefits: Uncovers hidden insights in large ("terabyte") datasets, beneficial for individuals, organizations, and society.

Concepts:

Multivariate analysis is an extension of bivariate analysis for multidimensional data. It uses techniques like heat maps for correlations. "A picture is worth a thousand words" – visualizations help depict multi-variable datasets effectively.

Multivariate vs. Multidimensional: Multivariate refers to multiple variables (often outcomes); multidimensional describes data requiring multiple dimensions to represent (typically vector spaces).

Example: Analyzing Instagram usage and self-esteem – bivariate ignores factors like age; multivariate includes them for comprehensive insights.

Text Visualization

Definition and Overview

Text Visualization is the graphical representation of qualitative text data, such as keywords or customer feedback, emphasizing frequency and patterns. It highlights frequently appearing words to provide a quick "gist" of documents or collections.

Key Points:

  • Purpose: Understanding and summarization (get the "gist"), grouping (cluster for overview/classification), comparison (document collections/evolution over time), insight (hidden trends/patterns), correlation (with other data like social networks).
  • Why Visualize Text: Reveals audience thoughts, quick and informative, extracts exciting/emotional information, visually engaging.
  • Data Sources: Documents (articles, books, novels, emails, web pages, blogs, tags, comments, programs, logs); Collections (messages, social networks, academic collaborations).
  • Challenges: High dimensionality (use text to represent text, select descriptive terms), context & semantics (provide source access), modeling abstraction (match analysis task with models, use progressive disclosure for levels from overview to details).

Text as Data: Treats text for research/analysis. Words as nominal data with meanings/relations (correlations: e.g., Hong Kong/San Francisco/Bay Area; order: months; membership: sports/hobbies; hierarchy, antonyms/synonyms, entities). Limitations: Frequency statistics ignore grammar, position, entities.

Example: Health Care Reform – Visualize news articles, speech transcripts, legal documents to answer questions about history (Clinton/Obama initiatives), compare keywords over time.

Text Processing Techniques

  • Tokenization: Breaks text into units (word-level: words; character-level: characters; subword: parts). Example: "This product is amazing, but the delivery was late." → ["This", "product", "is", "amazing", ",", "but", "the", "delivery", "was", "late", "."] (word-level). "Geeksforgeeks ! is-best, for. @geeks don’t" → ["geeksforgeeks", "!", "is", "-", "best", "for", "@", "geeks"] (subword).
  • Stemming: Reduces words to root form (e.g., "likes" → "like", "running" → "run").
  • Bag of Words (BoW): Represents text as vector of term frequencies, ignoring order. Document ≈ vector; document-term matrix. Example: Doc1: "John likes to watch movies and also likes football" → [1,2,1,1,1,0,0] for [john, likes, watch, movies, football, mary, games]. Limitations: Ignores grammar/semantics.

Python Example for BoW:

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import CountVectorizer
corpus = ['John likes to watch movies and also likes football', 'Mary also likes to watch football games']
vectorizer = CountVectorizer()
X = vectorizer.fit_transform(corpus)
print(vectorizer.get_feature_names_out())
print(X.toarray())

Keyword Weighting: TF-IDF

TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) measures word importance in a document relative to a corpus, downweighting common terms.

Components:

  • Term Frequency (TF): Occurrences of term t in document d divided by total words in d. Variants: log(1 + TF), proportion (TF / total).
  • Inverse Document Frequency (IDF): log(N / df(t)), where N = total documents, df(t) = documents containing t. Measures rarity.
  • TF-IDF = TF × IDF (often log-normalized).

Scenario:

Corpus (3 documents):

  1. "The quick brown fox quick." (5 words)
  2. "The lazy dog is quick." (5 words)
  3. "Brown fox and lazy dog." (5 words)

Task: Calculate TF-IDF for "quick".

Step 1: TF

  • Doc1: 2/5 = 0.4
  • Doc2: 1/5 = 0.2
  • Doc3: 0/5 = 0

Step 2: IDF

  • N = 3, df("quick") = 2
  • IDF = log(3/2) = log(1.5) ≈ 0.176

Step 3: TF-IDF (using log(1 + TF) × IDF)

  • Doc1: log(1 + 0.4) × 0.176 ≈ 0.146 × 0.176 ≈ 0.0257
  • Doc2: log(1 + 0.2) × 0.176 ≈ 0.079 × 0.176 ≈ 0.0139
  • Doc3: 0

Conclusion: "quick" is more important in Doc1 than Doc2, rare in corpus.

Python Example:

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
corpus = ["The quick brown fox quick.", "The lazy dog is quick.", "Brown fox and lazy dog."]
vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer()
X = vectorizer.fit_transform(corpus)
print(vectorizer.get_feature_names_out())
print(X.toarray())

Definition and Fundamentals

Time Series Data consists of an ordered sequence of timestamp values taken at equally spaced intervals. Unlike other data types, it is chronologically ordered, where each data point represents a specific moment in time, such as hourly, daily, or monthly measurements.

Key Points:

  • Applications: Used in sales forecasting, utility studies, budget analysis, economic forecasting, and inventory studies.
  • Characteristics: Includes trends, seasonality (repeating patterns), outliers, and abrupt changes.

Answer: plt.hist(ages, bins=10); plt.xlabel('Age'); plt.ylabel('Frequency'); plt.title('Age Distribution'); plt.show()

5. Product Sales Across Regions

Answer: Grouped bar chart or stacked bar chart - allows comparison of multiple products across different regions simultaneously.

6. Project Tasks with Start/End Dates

Answer: Gantt chart using plt.barh(tasks, durations, left=start_dates); plt.xlabel('Timeline'); plt.ylabel('Tasks'); plt.show()

7. Visual Encoding for Quantitative Data

Answer: Use position or length for quantitative; e.g., bar height in bar chart.

8. Channel Selection for Categorical

Answer: Use color hue or shape; avoid implying order with size or position.
